# Task 03 - Proportionality in Gravitation

## Problem Statement

Consider the Universal Law of Gravitation:

$$
F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}
$$

Determine the factor by which the force $F$ changes if the distance $r$ is doubled and both masses $m_1$ and $m_2$ are halved.

## Theory

The gravitational force is directly proportional to the product of the masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance:

$$
F \propto \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}
$$

Therefore, proportional changes can be analyzed by replacing the variables with their new values and comparing the new force with the original one.

## Step-by-Step Solution

The original force is

$$
F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}
$$

After the change:

$$
m_1' = \frac{m_1}{2}
$$

$$
m_2' = \frac{m_2}{2}
$$

$$
r' = 2r
$$

The new force is

$$
F' = G \frac{m_1' m_2'}{(r')^2}
$$

Substitute the changed quantities:

$$
F' = G \frac{\left(\frac{m_1}{2}\right)\left(\frac{m_2}{2}\right)}{(2r)^2}
$$

Simplify the numerator:

$$
\left(\frac{m_1}{2}\right)\left(\frac{m_2}{2}\right) = \frac{m_1 m_2}{4}
$$

Simplify the denominator:

$$
(2r)^2 = 4r^2
$$

Therefore,

$$
F' = G \frac{\frac{m_1 m_2}{4}}{4r^2}
$$

$$
F' = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{16r^2}
$$

Compare this with the original formula:

$$
F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}
$$

Hence,

$$
F' = \frac{1}{16} F
$$

## Final Result

The gravitational force becomes

$$
F' = \frac{F}{16}
$$

So the force decreases by a factor of

$$
16
$$

## Interpretation

Halving both masses reduces the force by a factor of $4$, because the product $m_1 m_2$ becomes four times smaller. Doubling the distance increases $r^2$ by a factor of $4$, which reduces the force by another factor of $4$. The total reduction is therefore $4 \cdot 4 = 16$.
